# KPOP Collect — Dev Log

A running log of design and development sessions, written for a portfolio case study audience.

## 2026-06-17 — Research Session: Game Design + Monetization Strategy

Today's session was focused on locking down three foundational decisions that will shape how KPOP Collect scales beyond a prototype: the level architecture, the difficulty curve, and the monetization loop. These are the kinds of decisions that feel low-stakes early on but become very expensive to change once the game is in players' hands, so it was worth taking the time to reason through them carefully.

**Unlimited levels via procedural difficulty.** Rather than hand-crafting individual puzzles, the level system will be parameterized by three variables: number of groups (3–5), cards per group (3–4), and number of starting empty holders (1–2). Easy levels give players breathing room — fewer groups, an extra empty holder for maneuvering. Hard levels pack in 4–5 groups against a single empty slot, making every move feel load-bearing. Because difficulty is a combination of these variables rather than a fixed set of puzzles, the game can generate effectively unlimited levels without the design overhead of crafting each one individually. The tradeoff is that pure procedural generation doesn't guarantee elegance — some future work will be needed to define what makes a "good" puzzle solvable, not just technically completable.

**The wave pattern for difficulty sequencing.** Rather than a linear difficulty ramp (which burns players out) or random difficulty (which feels unfair), the game will use a wave pattern: 3 Easy → 2 Moderate → 1 Hard, repeating, with the baseline rising each full cycle. This is borrowed from the playbook of genre leaders like Magic Sort: players feel progress even when the wave resets, because "easy" at level 90 is harder than "easy" at level 10. The wave structure also creates natural anticipation — players who clear two moderate levels know a hard level is coming and are mentally prepared, which makes the difficulty spike feel earned rather than punishing.

**Three monetization triggers, each timed to emotional state.** The most important product insight from analyzing Magic Sort's monetization model is that *when* you make the offer matters as much as *what* you offer. We've designed three purchase moments, each timed to a distinct emotional state: (1) **+1 Holder at $0.99** — offered when a player has been stuck for 30+ seconds on a hard level, at peak frustration and the feeling of being one move away from a solution; (2) **Streak Saver at $0.99** — triggered on the "Restart" path when a player with a win streak decides to bail on a stuck level; loss aversion is one of the most reliable conversion triggers in mobile gaming; (3) **Guaranteed Legendary Photocard at $0.99** — offered as a side branch after clearing a hard level, timed to the dopamine peak of a difficult win. Players who've just overcome something hard feel rewarded and are primed to extend that feeling. The $0.99 price point is deliberate across all three — impulse-buy friction is near zero, and the first purchase is the most important one to capture.

**Artifact produced.** All three systems are now documented in the FigJam game flowchart, color-coded by function: blue for onboarding, pink for core decision hubs, violet for player actions, green for win states, red for rejection/stuck paths, yellow for all monetization trigger points. The chart now shows the full game loop from App Launch through the Difficulty Engine, Core Sorting Loop, Win/Stuck branching, Gacha Pack Flow, and Binder Collection — including the three $0.99 offer moments and how they re-enter the main loop. This will serve as the source of truth for the prototype's next iteration and eventually the spec for a React Native implementation.
